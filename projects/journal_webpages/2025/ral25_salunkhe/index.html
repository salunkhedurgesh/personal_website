Cuspidal Redundant Robots: Classification of Infinitely Many IKS of Special Classes of 7R Robots

Durgesh Haribhau Salunkhe, Sthithpragya Gupta, Aude Billard

DOI: 10.1109/LRA.2025.3623011  ·  pp. 12509–12516

Key contributions

  • 01. First systematic cuspidality classification framework for 7R wrist-partitioned redundant robots, filling a gap left open by decades of work on 3R and 6R architectures.
  • 02. A reduction method that maps any 7R robot to a parametrized family of 3R robots via the redundant joint angle, enabling direct application of established cuspidality criteria.
  • 03. Analysis of commercially available cobots showing that design offsets can induce cuspidality — with practical guidelines for designing non-cuspidal redundant robots.

Abstract

Redundant robots, with more degrees of freedom than required for a given task, offer enhanced dexterity but can exhibit complex kinematic behaviour in motion planning. Cuspidal robots, which can change inverse kinematic solutions without crossing singularities, have been reported to pose unique challenges for motion feasibility and repeatability. While cuspidality has been extensively studied for 3R and certain 6R robots, no formal classification exists for redundant architectures. This letter presents a systematic framework for classifying 7R wrist-partitioned redundant robots based on their cuspidal properties. The method reduces the 7R structure to a parameterized 3R equivalent via the redundant joint angle, enabling the application of established theory for cuspidal robots. Using this approach, commercially available robots are analysed and categorized as cuspidal or noncuspidal. Results show that the design offsets in commercial cobots may lead to cuspidality, which can potentially cause a nonsingular change of operation mode in collaborative applications. This classification framework provides a foundation for cuspidality-aware path planning and offers practical guidelines for designing non-cuspidal redundant robots to ensure safer and more predictable operation.

FAQ

What is a cuspidal robot? ▼
A cuspidal robot can move from one inverse kinematic solution (IKS) to another without crossing a singularity — something previously thought impossible. This happens because the robot has multiple IKS within a singularity-free region of its workspace. The name comes from a characteristic cusp point in the workspace boundary.
Why is cuspidality a problem for redundant robots? ▼
Redundant robots (more DoF than needed) use a self-motion parameter to resolve redundancy. If the robot is cuspidal for certain values of this parameter, the motion planner may silently switch the robot into a different IKS, causing unexpected joint motions, potential collisions with the environment, and loss of repeatability — especially dangerous in collaborative applications.
Which commercial cobots are affected? ▼
The paper analyses wrist-partitioned 7R cobots currently deployed in industry. Results show that non-zero link offsets — common in modern cobot designs — are a key indicator of cuspidality. Specific commercial robots are classified in the paper; please refer to Table II for the full list.
How can robot designers avoid cuspidality? ▼
The paper provides concrete geometric guidelines: minimizing or zeroing specific link offsets in the wrist-partitioned 7R structure is the primary lever. The classification framework can be applied at the design stage — before the robot is built — to verify that no self-motion value leads to a cuspidal 3R sub-problem.
